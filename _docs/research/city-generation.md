## Constraint-driven generation algorithms

The most durable base model is still the **street-first, lot-second, building-third** family of generators. Parish and M├╝llerŌĆÖs SIGGRAPH system remains foundational because it takes input maps such as land-water boundaries and population density, then generates highways and streets, subdivides land into lots, and creates building geometry on those allotments; crucially, their L-system extensions explicitly support both **global goals and local constraints**. That makes this approach a good starting point when the city must respond to terrain, coastlines, population centers, or designer-painted masks rather than just emit a random urban texture. Where you need stronger **interactive author control** over the street network, tensor-field methods are a better fit than raw L-systems alone. ## Terrain-aware siting and local terrain modification

Terrain adaptation is one of the clearest places where the research strongly matches your idea of local ŌĆ£spheresŌĆØ or other modifiers. Emilien, Bernhardt, Peytavie, Cani, and GalinŌĆÖs village generator on arbitrary terrains is especially relevant because it does not assume flat land: it uses **interest maps** to place settlement seeds and roads, segments land into parcels, and then uses an **open shape grammar** to generate houses adapted to local slope. Their paper is valuable not because you want villages, but because it demonstrates a mature workflow for settlement generation on rough terrain: first score terrain suitability, then create roads and parcels, then adapt building geometry to the grading budget that remains. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn10view6Ņłü

For roads across uneven planetary terrain, Galin and colleagues provide a strong algorithmic template. Their road generator formulates the problem as a **weighted anisotropic shortest path**, minimizing a cost field that includes slope and natural obstacles such as rivers, lakes, mountains, and forests; the system can also segment the result into surface roads, tunnels, and bridges and excavate terrain along the path. This is a very good fit for roads between a starport, a trade district, a mining area, and a warehouse zone, because it lets you treat roads as solutions to terrain and gameplay costs rather than as decorative splines drawn after the city already exists. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn8view2Ņłü

Your proposed terrain-modifier layer maps naturally onto two implementation families. In a **heightmap** workflow, local flattening, benched pads, and embankments can be implemented with stamp-style operations. UnityŌĆÖs Terrain Tools documentation is a clear example: the Stamp Terrain tool can apply a brush or mesh to the terrain heightmap, with behaviors such as set, raise, lower, and min, and it supports interactive placement gizmos. The limitation is equally important: because the terrain is heightmap-based, concave or hole-rich forms are not represented well. So if your planetary terrain is fundamentally a heightfield, local ŌĆ£city foundation spheresŌĆØ or ŌĆ£flatten padsŌĆØ are practical and cheap, but they remain surface deformation, not volumetric editing. If you need deeper editingŌĆösmooth cuts, retaining volumes, local fills, or future support for caves and undercutsŌĆöthen a **signed distance field** layer is the more general approach. Voxel ToolsŌĆÖ smooth-terrain documentation explains why SDFs are useful: instead of storing binary occupied/empty values, the terrain stores a signed distance to the surface, which enables smooth curved surfaces and local edits. A particularly useful conceptual bridge is the **semantic-constraint** literature. Their examples are line-of-sight and choke points, but the same machinery applies cleanly to city siting: a starport pad constraint can claim highest priority over local terrain roughness; a waterfront market constraint can preserve shoreline access; a slum district can be prevented from occupying the same extent as a secure customs zone; and if the terrain or another feature changes, the constrained region can be re-evaluated rather than regenerated globally. ## Planet rendering and editor integration

Because you mentioned an already-rendered planet, the rendering side matters as much as the generation side. If the existing planet is a **heightfield planet** rather than a fully volumetric world, then the most research-aligned large-scale renderers are still **clipmaps and quadtree hierarchies**. Losasso and HoppeŌĆÖs geometry clipmaps cache nested regular grids around the viewer, support incremental updates, and can synthesize finer terrain with fractal detail near the viewer; the GPU-based version moves most computation onto the GPU and was explicitly designed for very large terrains. Spherical clipmaps adapt the same idea to spherical terrains by displacing static grids with height maps, and ellipsoidal variants extend that to geodetically accurate planetary surfaces. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn10view4Ņłéturn10view5Ņłéturn13search0Ņłü

For a modern planet renderer, **Ellipsoidal Cube Maps** are especially relevant if you need precision and quadtree-friendly storage. Lambers and KolbŌĆÖs ECM approach stores planetary terrain data in **quadtree hierarchies**, uses an equal-area map projection that avoids severe pole degradation, and explicitly addresses numerical-accuracy limitations so rendering can remain artifact-free in single-precision pipelines. Their reference renderer also supports asynchronous caching across disk, memory, and GPU memory and can combine elevation and texture datasets on the GPU at render time. ## Sundog-informed city content and architectural direction

If you want cities that reference **SunDog: Frozen Legacy** while becoming deeper and more varied, the original game gives you a surprisingly strong functional template. The Atari ST manual describes a region with **over 50 cities on 18 planets**, travel within cities and across continents, trade exchanges in each city, warehouses attached to those exchanges, banking via Unitellers in every city, and a logistics loop built around cargo, fuel, ship repair, and the retrieval of cryogens from scattered warehouses. The manual also frames bars as information-bearing social spaces, and the broader gameplay descriptions note ship-parts shops, restaurants/bars, hotels, and banks as recurring city functions. The original game also matters stylistically. ## Recommended pipeline for this project

The best-fit production pipeline is an **interactive, local, constraint-first city generator** layered on top of your existing planet terrain. In the viewport, the user places a candidate city center or starport anchor. The tool immediately samples local terrain into height, slope, water, and accessibility maps, much like the terrain-analysis phases used in successful terrain-adaptive settlement systems. It then computes a site score and previews which macro-patterns are viable: harbor city, plateau city, canyon city, mining outpost, hillside slum belt, and so on. If the terrain is stable, this preview does not need to regenerate the full city; it only needs to expose the local constraint envelope. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn25view0Ņłéturn17view0Ņłéturn16view0Ņłü

Once a site is accepted, apply a **modifier stack** before urban generation. On a heightfield planet, this should be a layered stamp system: first the major flatten or bench for the starport and city core, then road cuts and fills, then drainage or shoreline adjustments, then texture/material overrides. Where the flatten budget is intentionally limited, let the settlement remain partially terraced and rely on slope-adaptive parceling and building grammar rather than over-flattening everything. If you want more freedom later, move the modifier stack to an SDF overlay so the local city foundation can be expressed with spheres, boxes, and unions on GPU, then baked or unified after editing. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn10view3Ņłéturn29view0Ņłéturn29view1Ņłéturn10view6Ņłü

For the road and block skeleton, use **two different algorithms for two different jobs**. Use tensor fields or graph growth for the **internal city network**, because they offer strong editorial control and fit stylized historical or social variation. Use anisotropic shortest-path planning for the **terrain-forced connectors**ŌĆöthe roads that must reach an existing highway, mine, coastal dock, observatory, or secondary warehouse across difficult terrain. Then convert enclosed graphs into blocks and lots, exactly as CityEngine-style graph workflows do, because graph-derived lots are easier to edit, regenerate, and align with gameplay metadata than free-floating building footprints. At the district level, combine **land-use attraction/repulsion** with **hard service requirements**. The land-use layer decides the macro-zonesŌĆöindustrial, commercial, high-, middle-, and low-class residential, slums, civic, green, transportŌĆöusing district relationships and terrain suitability. The service layer then solves for mandatory facilities and adjacency rules: every trade city needs an exchange and warehouse; every starport city needs ship-parts and repair; information economy districts need bars or analogous rumor hubs; banks and civic/security services must be reachable; and some facilities should be unique or capped per city. This is where a declarative CSP or ASP layer earns its keep, because it gives you guarantees that a city will never ship without the facilities needed by the game loop. For buildings, use a **style grammar with district families**, not a single global kit. CGA-style grammar systems remain one of the best ways to produce architectural variety without losing control, and the CityEngine ecosystem shows that such rules can also encode zoning envelopes and parcel-specific dimensional rules. For your project, that should become a family system: a shared ŌĆ£SunDog languageŌĆØ of rectilinear modules, strong signage/icon cues, vivid landmark color accents, and compact readable masses, then district-specific rule sets layered on top for affluent, middle, industrial, frontier, or slum variants. Finally, bake the output into the planet renderer as **multi-scale city data**, not just meshes. Near the player, render detailed roads, instances, fa├¦ades, signage, interiors, and local terrain cuts. At middle distance, favor instanced masses, road ribbons, and district impostors. At far distance or from orbit, use semantic masks, low-frequency height fields, emissive night-light layers, and landmark meshes only. That multi-scale representation is consistent with modern city-layout work that separates semantic fields from height fields, and with clipmap-style planetary rendering where locality and streaming matter more than globally mutable meshes.
